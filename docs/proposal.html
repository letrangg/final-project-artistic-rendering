Title, Summary and Team Members

Our project focuses on creating a drawing software that can simulate real life effects of applying a single medium (out of a selection of mediums) on a piece of paper or other materials (canvas, paper[a], wood, silk,...) so that the artists could create painting effects similar to that in real life.

Our team has three members: Richard Chen, Dahyun Kim, and Trang Le

Problem Description

As we have seen in common drawing softwares such as Photoshop and Clip Studio paint, or other tablet applications that enable digital drawing such as Procreate, there are different brush modes such as watercolor or paint, letting users draw on a new layer with brush strokes that look like that of a paint brush. However, these effects still look quite 2D, as there is no interaction between the background paper and the brushstroke, so the result would look like a 2D rasterized image of a brush stroke on a blank layer. For this reason, many digital artists have created paintings with these drawing tools, and the results, though breathtaking, could show that they look a bit “digital,” or “computer-produced,” instead of having the roughness and natural look of traditional materials such as oil, watercolor, and pastels. To address this problem, we want to create a software that simulate real physical interactions between the medium (for example, oil) and the background material (for example, canvas), to create lifelike paintings that clearly show the materials used. It is important to solve these issues, as digital artists have strived to create digital art that looks like painting in real life.

One of the challenges is that the roughness of the material is hard to simulate, and would take up memory and slow down the program. As we can see in the two paintings below, the artist tries to paint the characters with as much roughness as it would be in real life, and the first picture seems to have more coarseness than the second picture.

One of the other things to consider is that color blending is different in different materials. For paint (in this case we’ll assume the paint dries the moment it touches the layer), when we paste in a new color on top of another, it overlays the old color without the two colors being mixed together. Whereas for watercolor and chalk pastel, we cannot “fix” the color once it is pasted onto the layer. The old layer of color will mix with the new one when we add another level of color. Below images are examples in which color blending works differently for different materials (paint has colors that can overlay on top of each other, while for watercolor, the layers will blend into each other so we have to be careful when adding a new color to an already existing color).

        To solve these problems, first we limit the number of materials into three categories: watercolor, paint, and chalk pastel (which is a bit powdery and chalky, compared to the other two smooth medium). Each of them would have different physics when interacting with its corresponding surface. We would then dig deep into how the physics of chalk, oil, and water works when interacting with their surfaces. For this, we would need to look at the constant for material modelling and how the stiffness of the surface may affect the way color is applied to it.

Goals and Deliverables:

What we plan to deliver:

Our goal is to create “realistic-looking” art, or realistic simulations of various types of pigments, on varied surface materials. Our baseline goal would be to properly simulate the effects of at least one of the three materials above (watercolor, paint, chalk pastel). Other criteria include real-time rendering capabilities (frame rates 20~30 fps) and high enough resolution for photorealism (720p+) with the ability to zoom in closer[b][c].

Demos should allow user to pick any of the supported surfaces and any supported type of pigment, and apply the pigment to the surface using a mouse. Ideally, the user should be able to control application amount using speed or some other measurements of pressure. User might also be able to see specific interactions with the surface, depending on its qualities, as well as effects over time (either short timeframe or long). In the case of a long-term effect like paint drying, we would want a “speedup” option.

What we hope to deliver: 

We hope to deliver a full package for any beginner artist wanting to learn the basics of new materials. We hope to have fully functioning tools that support oil, watercolor and chalk pastel, and the surfaces they interact with would correspond to canvas and watercolor paper.

The end goal is to support all three materials (watercolor, paint, chalk pastel) as well as a number of tool simulations for variety of painting choices. The end result would somewhat resemble the below images, where you can clearly see the texture of the surface, the texture of the medium, and some interesting interactions between them.

Unexpected issues could include being unable to achieve a satisfactory frame rate, not being able to find information on how to simulate surfaces or surface-medium interactions, [d]slow rendering due to accuracy of the physical behaviour of materials.

We would measure the quality of our system based on the response time of each stroke (assuming that we choose the certain size of the canvas to enables good performance), how much does the stroke interact with the canvas and with each other, and how accurate the color mixture will be.

The question we are planning to answer is that, what stops software companies from making realistic art softwares? What hinders the most popular drawing softwares from enabling a feature similar to that of a real life painting?
