Our project focuses on creating a drawing software that can simulate real life effects of applying a single medium (out of a selection of mediums) on a piece of paper or other materials (canvas, fabric, wood, silk,...) so that the artists could create painting effects similar to that in real life.

Members: Trang Le, Richard Chen, Dahyun Kim

Problem Description

As we have seen in common drawing softwares such as Photoshop and Clip Studio paint, or other tablet applications that enable digital drawing such as Procreate, there are different brush modes such as watercolor or paint, letting users to draw on a blank layer with brush strokes that look like that of a paint brush. However, these effects still look pretty 2D, as there is no interaction between the background and the stroke, and it looks like the stroke is just a 2D rasterized image of a brush stroke on a blank layer. For this reason, many digital artists have created paintings with these drawing tools, and the result, though breathtaking, could show that it looks a bit “digital,” or “computer-produced,” instead of showing the roughness and natural look of traditional materials such as oil, watercolor, and pastels. To address this problem, we want to create a software that simulate real physical interactions between the medium (for example, oil) and the background material (for example, canvas), to create lifelike paintings that clearly show the materials used. It is important to solve these issues, as digital artists have strived to create digital art that looks like painting in real life.

One of the challenges is that the roughness of the material is hard to simulate, and would take up memory and slow down the program. As we can see in the two paintings below, the artist tries to paint the characters with as much roughness as it would be in real life, and the first picture seems to have more coarseness than the second picture.

One of the other things to consider is that color blending is different in different materials. For paint (in this case we’ll assume the paint dries the moment it touches the layer), when we paste in a new color on top of another, it overlays the old color without the two colors being mixed together. Whereas for watercolor and chalk pastel, we cannot “fix” the color once it is pasted onto the layer. The color will remain there, and the only way to change is to mix white or a different color on top to create a new color.

        To solve these problems, first we limit the number of materials into three categories: watercolor, paint, and chalk pastel (which is a bit powdery and chalky, compared to the other two smooth medium). Each of them would have different physics when interacting with its corresponding surface.

Goals and Deliverables:

Realistic simulations of various types of pigments, on varied surface materials. The end goal is something like the below images, where you can clearly see the texture of the surface, the texture of the medium, and some interesting interactions between them.

Demos should allow user to pick any of the supported surfaces and any supported type of pigment, and apply the pigment to the surface using a mouse[a]. Ideally, the user should be able to control application amount using speed or some other measurement of pressure. User might also be able to see specific interactions with the surface, depending on its qualities, as well as effects over time (either short timeframe or long). In the case of a long-term effect like paint drying, we would want a “speedup” option

We aim for reasonable real-time frame rates (>20-30 fps), as well as high enough resolution to mimic photorealism (720p+)

Unexpected issues could include being unable to achieve a satisfactory frame rate, not being able to find information on how to simulate surfaces or surface-medium interactions, [b]
